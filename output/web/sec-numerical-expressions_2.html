    Section 1.1 Numerical Expressions

    Objectives

    • Write numerical expressions from verbal expressions.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Evaluate numerical expressions using the order of operations.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Apply numerical expressions to solve real-world problems.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    This section covers the fundamentals of writing, evaluating, and applying numerical expressions.
    🔗
    Suppose your kitchen sink has sprung a leak. You hire a plumber who charges a flat $50 service fee plus $75 per hour of labor. If the plumber works for 3 hours, how much will you owe?
    🔗
    To calculate the total cost, you could write the following mathematical expression:
    \begin{equation} 50 + 75 \cdot 3\tag{1.1.1} \end{equation}
    Evaluating this expression gives the total cost of $275.
    🔗
    The above expression (1.1.1) is an example of a numerical expression.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.1.

    A numerical expression is an expression formed only from numbers and the basic operations of arithmetic. Numerical expressions may also contain grouping symbols like: \((...), [...], \{...\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.2.

    The following are additional examples of numerical expressions:
    \begin{align*} & 5 \cdot 3 - 2\\ & 10 + 7 \cdot 2\\ & (8-3)^2 + 6 \div 3\\ & 2 \cdot 3 + 4 \cdot 5 - 6 \div 2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    In this section, we will learn how to write numerical expressions from their verbal descriptions, evaluate them using the order of operations, and apply them to solve real-world problems.
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.1 Learn: Writing Numerical Expressions

    Subsubsection 1.1.1.1 Translate a Verbal Expression without Grouping Symbols

    The verbal expression "twelve divided by four plus two" can be translated into the numerical expression:
    \begin{equation*} 12 \div 4 + 2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In general, we translate simple verbal expressions into numerical expressions as follows:
    🔗
    • "five plus three" \(\rightarrow 5 + 3 \) (Addition)
      🔗
      🔗
    • "five minus three" \(\rightarrow 5 - 3 \) (Subtraction)
      🔗
      🔗
    • "five times three" \(\rightarrow 5 \cdot 3 \) (Multiplication)
      🔗
      🔗
    • "five divided by three" \(\rightarrow 5 \div 3 \) or \(\frac{5}{3} \) (Division)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Checkpoint 1.1.3. Translate a Verbal Expression.
    Translate the verbal expression "eight minus three times two" into a numerical expression.
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(8 - 3 \cdot 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 1.1.1.2 Translate a Verbal Expression with Grouping Symbols

    Some verbal expressions contain special phrases that refer to the result of a basic arithmetic operations. Such expressions require the use of grouping symbols to properly translate them into numerical expressions.
    🔗
    For example, the verbal expression "the sum of five and three times two" is translated into the numerical expression:
    \begin{equation} (5 + 3) \cdot 2\tag{1.1.2} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Note in (1.1.2) above how we have enclosed the addition operation in parentheses: "the sum of five and three" becomes \((5 + 3)\text{.}\) This is necessary, because when we speak of the "sum" of two numbers, we want to compute with the output of that operation rather than any of its internal parts.
    🔗
    In general, we translate verbal expressions that require grouping symbols as follows:
    • "the sum of" refers to the result of addition \((\_\_+\_\_)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • "the difference of" refers to the result of subtraction \((\_\_-\_\_)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • "the product of" refers to the result of multiplication \((\_\_\cdot\_\_)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • "the quotient of" refers to the result of division \((\frac{\;\;\;}{\;\;\;})\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Example 1.1.4. Translate a Verbal Expression with Grouping Symbols.
    Write a numerical expression for each of the following verbal expressions:
    1. "two times the difference of six and four".
      🔗
      🔗
    2. "twelve divided by the sum of four and two".
      🔗
      🔗
    3. "the sum of eight and seven divided by the difference of four and one".
      🔗
      🔗
    4. "the difference of nine and five times the sum of three and two".
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Answer.
    1. \(2 \cdot (6 - 4)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(12 \div (4 + 2)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \((8 + 7) \div (4 - 1)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \((9 - 5) \cdot (3 + 2)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 1.1.2 Learn: Evaluating Numerical Expressions

    We have learned how to write numerical expressions from verbal descriptions. But what do such expressions actually mean?
    🔗
    Recall the plumbing problem from above. Suppose you were to hand your friend a slip of paper with just the above expression (1.1.1) written on it. Without knowing anything about how you were using this expression, your friend should still be able to correctly interpret and evaluate it to get its value of 275.
    🔗
    That is the power of mathematical language: it allows us to communicate ideas precisely and unambiguously. Like all mathematical language, numerical expressions are powerful because they communicate a precise meaning independent from any particular problem they model.
    🔗
    For numerical expressions, the order of operations tells us the proper sequence for combining the numbers in the expression using the given operations. In this way, we can evaluate every numerical expression to a single numerical value.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.5.

    The order of operations gives the proper order to evaluate any numerical expression:
    1. First, evaluate inside parentheses (...) and other grouping symbols.
      🔗
    2. Next, evaluate exponents (powers and roots or radicals).
      🔗
    3. Then, perform multiplication and division from left to right.
      🔗
    4. Finally, perform addition and subtraction from left to right.
      🔗
    Note: Multiplication does not always come before Division, and Addition does not always come before Subtraction. They are performed from left to right as they appear in the expression.
    🔗
    The above order of operations can be remembered using the acronym PEMDAS, which stands for Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.6. Evaluate numerical expressions using the order of operations.

    Use the order of operations to evaluate the numerical expressions from Example 1.1.2
    \begin{align*} & 5 /cdot 3 - 2 = \_\_\_\\ & 10 + 7 \cdot 2 = \_\_\_\\ & (8-3)^2 + 6 \div 3 = \_\_\_\\ & 2 \cdot 3 + 4 \cdot 5 - 6 \div 2 = \_\_\_ \end{align*}
    🔗
    Answer.
    \begin{align*} & 5 \cdot 3 - 2 = 13\\ & 10 + 7 \cdot 2 = 24\\ & (8-3)^2 + 6 \div 3 = 27 \\ & 2 \cdot 3 + 4 \cdot 5 - 6 \div 2 = 23 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    For example, when evaluating the expression
    \begin{equation*} 5 + 3^2 \cdot (4-1) \end{equation*}
    we follow the order of operations as shown below:
    \begin{align*} \amp 5 + 3^2 \cdot (4-1) =\\ \amp 5 + 3^2 \cdot 3 =\\ \amp 5 + 9 \cdot 3 =\\ \amp 5 + 27 =\\ \amp 32 \end{align*}
    First, we evaluate inside the parentheses. Next, we compute the exponent. Then, we multiply. Finally, we add and get the expression’s value of \(32\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 1.1.7. Evaluate a Numerical Expression.

    Evaluate the expression \(2^2 - 10 \div 5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We first evaluate the exponent \(2^2 = 4\text{,}\) then perform the division \(10 \div 5 = 2\text{,}\) and finally subtract these two results to get \(4 - 2 = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.8. Evaluate a Numerical Expression with Grouping Symbols.

    Evaluate the expression \((2 + 3) \cdot (4 - 1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    We first evaluate the two expressions in parentheses to get \(5\) and \(3\text{,}\) respectively, and then multiply these results to get \(15\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.1.9. Write and Evaluate a Numerical Expression.

    Translate and evaluate the verbal expression "the difference of 10 and 4 multiplied by 2."
    🔗
    Answer.
    We write this expression as \((10 - 4) \cdot 2\) and evaluate it to get \(6 \cdot 2 = 12\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.1.10. Evaluate Numerical Expressions.

    Evaluate the following numerical expressions using the order of operations:
    \begin{align*} & 8 + 2^3 - 4 \div 2\\ & (6 + 2) \cdot 3 - 5\\ & 12 \div (3 + 1) + 2^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Answer.
    The results are:
    \begin{align*} & 8 + 8 - 2 = 14\\ & 8 \cdot 3 - 5 = 19\\ & 12 \div 4 + 4 = 7 \end{align*}

    Subsection 1.1.3 Learn: Plan for Problem Solving

    To solve real-world problems, we often need to write numerical expressions that model the situation. The key steps are:
    1. Identify the quantities involved.
      🔗
    2. Determine the operations needed to relate these quantities.
      🔗
    3. Write a numerical expression that captures the relationships.
      🔗
    4. Evaluate the expression to find the solution.
      🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 1.1.11. Write and Evaluate Expressions.

    Write a numerical expression for the following problem: "A store sells apples for $2 each and oranges for $3 each. If you buy 4 apples and 5 oranges, how much do you spend in total?"
    🔗
    Answer.
    The numerical expression is \(4 \cdot 2 + 5 \cdot 3\text{,}\) which evaluates to $23.
    🔗
    🔗
